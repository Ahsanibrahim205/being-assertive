Ten steps to being assertive

Step 1:

List and describe specific situations which are accompanied by feelings of being ill-at-ease, guilty of self-doubt or where you feel that you have been ‘put upon’ and forced to do something against your will.

This involves listing the situations in which you feel ineffectual and where you behave submissively or non-assertively. You may have wished after the event that you had done something like these: expressing a difference of opinion, trying to discuss with uncooperative individuals, discussing how to resolve a problem or saying no to a request which imposes demands on you.

Step 2:

List the people who you have problems asserting yourself with, parents, spouse, employer, fellow team members or strangers.

Step 3:

Describe what precise key behaviours have inhibited you from speaking your mind or being assertiv

  • this could be wanting to be liked
  • not wanting to go against the dominant person
  • fear of being the only dissenting voice
  • feeling that your opinions do not count or are worthless and that no-one will listen
  • continually seeking approval before performing a task
  • compromising all the time or being unable to request new work schedules.

    • Behaviours Discomfort / 5
  • After completing such as list you can rate them on a scale of 1-5 in terms of their degree of discomfort or threat.

Step 4:

Choose a relevant situation and person which you have identified as having the potential to improve your wellness provided you can assert your viewpoint.

Step 5:

Prepare for the meeting in which you will be assertive.

Jot down in bullet-form about 3 or 4 central observations you have made

  • Summarise the facts as perceived by yourself.
  • This should include your thoughts about the situation, being careful not to make value judgements or assigning blame

An "I" message is a good way to let people know what you are thinking. It is made up of three parts.

  • Behaviour - what is it, exactly, that the other person has done or is doing?
  • Effect - what is happening because of their behaviour?
  • Feelings - what effect does their behaviour have on your feelings?

By using this kind of message, you are giving another person complete information, leaving no room for second-guessing or doubt.

An example: "When you come late to the meeting (behaviour) I feel angry (feelings) because we have to repeat information the rest of us heard (effect)."

This is much more productive and assertive than simply ignoring the problem or just expressing your anger or frustration.

Proceed to list the main feelings that you experienced beginning the sentences with “I” such as: “I feel disappointed”, I feel confused”, “I experience a tension in this situation”.

Then prepare your wishes and needs in a determined and easily understandable fashion such as: “I cannot invest more than 7 hours on this task”, “I do not want to work overtime”, and “I want my holidays to fit in with those of my children’s”.

Now go on to reveal the consequences of this desired co-operation

“If you agree to give me more time, we shall develop a more comprehensive marketing strategy for the sales of goods”, “By delegating some of this work onto my co-worker I can proceed to do the more complex tasks alone”

Step 6:

It is worth rehearsing this encounter before it happens. Try to go through in your mind all the possible outcomes (both positive and negative) and devise a strategy for dealing with them so that you are thoroughly prepared before the meeting.

Step 7:

Arrange a specific time and place to meet with the relevant person with whom you have not been assertive enough about your chosen situation.

Step 8:

Take your notes into the meeting and openly refer to them as you feel you have to – do not feel in any way obliged to conceal your notes / preparation. Make sure you cover all the points you want to.

Step 9:

When communicating with someone there are some golden rules

  • Do not use any submissive language.
  • Ensure your message is clear and concise
  • Control your voice and body language.
  • Speak clearly and steadily, try to keep the pitch and tone steady as well.
  • Once you have finished speaking – SHUT UP!
  • Do not fill silences or think you have to expand on what you have said.
  • Keep the small talk to a minimum in this instance.
  • You are there to deliver a message, do not dilute its’ impact

Step 10:

review how the meeting went in terms of

  • the outcomes you wanted v the outcomes you achieved
  • the way the meeting went
  • how you believe your relationship has changed with the other person (a) from your perspective and (b) from theirs
  • your ability to assert yourself.

Social relationships and negotiating is a bit like playing chess. Your opponent may pursue alternative strategies designed to prevent you from executing certain moves. They might well use various methods of manipulation to make you feel worse, guilty, anxious or irritated and try to get you to subsequently “reconsider” your position (and hence do what they want). It is important to keep in mind that another person is not able to “make” you feel any particular feeling. Your feelings are your choices in response to other people’s behaviour.

There are many methods of coping with such difficult persons including

  • defusing the situation by taking time-out
  • shifting the focus from the ‘content’ of the discussion to the ‘process’ of what is happening between the two of you.
  • two of the most favoured methods are called ‘conquering blocking manoeuvres’.

Method 1:

Broken record’ this is a bit like the old LP playing records in which the stylus gets stuck on a track and repeats the same section of the song, so you effectively “repeat”

  • your message clearly and concisely and unwavering over and over again without being influenced or unduly perturbed by the other’s demands. For example, “I’m sorry, but I will not take any more work on at this moment, I know that this will cause you a problem, but I do not have the time to fit anything else in without badly affecting my own work”. It is important to remember that whilst the message needs to remain constant you do not have to slavishly repeat word for word. Example “Right now my own workload is such that I don’t have the time to take on additional tasks.”

Method 2:

‘Fogging’, this is a tool for absorbing criticism without adopting an anxious and self-doubting attitude. It involves a calm and collected statement of endorsement of the other statements in the form that “there may be some truth in what you are saying” but you remain your own judge.
